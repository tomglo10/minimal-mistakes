### Understanding How Temperature Extremes Affect Heart Health
<p>Extreme temperatures, whether hot or cold, can affect the <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2024/06/is-heart-attack-risk-increased-more-by.md" title="Is Heart Attack Risk Increased More By" rel="dofollow">**risk of heart**</a> attacks. Research shows that cold weather carries a greater risk, often causing a noticeable rise in cases during the winter months. People, especially those with heart problems, should be cautious and take extra care in extreme weather.<span></span></p><!--more--><div style="margin: 30px 0px; text-align: center;"><img alt="A split-image representing the cardiovascular impact of extreme cold and extreme heat on heart health." border="0" data-original-height="532" data-original-width="945" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEiFuCntdjUCDHqrzhvnGnIM3Qh3Y48U8cR5UMxxzP1t6PFrnTiQHRngaM_83q9cCYN258n2qcr-53BbYFoRmIcvEE-FyWR4ZqDA9MnQRqMLe6_dwy72j0-uFAa9UBCulyoFMVYaf1jYm09oXHMA_ehECAK27LfNaNQzi_dRm3C4gMe68gi8UfTuKbOAffXv/s16000/Hot%20Cold.webp" style="border-radius: 12px; box-shadow: rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.1) 0px 4px 12px; max-width: 100%;" title="The cardiovascular impact of extreme cold and extreme heat on heart health." />
    <p style="color: #666666; font-size: 0.9rem; font-style: italic; margin-top: 10px;">Engaging in physical activity during both warm and chilly conditions can significantly contribute to maintaining and enhancing your cardiovascular health.</p></div>
<div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;">
Your heart works nonstop, like a powerful engine—but extreme heat and cold can put it under serious strain. In hot
weather, it pumps harder to cool you down; in cold, blood pressure rises as vessels tighten, increasing heart attack
risk. Older adults, those with heart disease, high blood pressure, or obesity are especially vulnerable. Stay safe by
staying hydrated, dressing for the weather, avoiding intense activity during temperature extremes, and checking in with
  your care team—your heart will thank you.</div>
<p></p>
<div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid rgb(13, 71, 161); border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;">
### Climate Change Adaptation and Cardiovascular Health
<p>
  Globally, cold temperatures have historically caused more cardiovascular deaths than heat—about 8.2% vs. 0.66%
  annually from 2000–2019, totaling 1.8 million deaths a year. But heat-related heart deaths are rising, signaling a
  shift as the planet warms. Effective climate adaptation must address both cold and heat risks to protect heart health
  now and in the future.
</p>
###A Study on global cardiovascular mortality burden
<p>
  High temperature exposure can cause conditions ranging from mild heat exhaustion to severe heat stroke, impacting
  vasodilation, body temperature control, metabolic rate, oxygen use, and electrolyte balance, which may exacerbate
  arrhythmias.</p><p><a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0735109724068918?via%3Dihub" target="_blank">The study</a>
  reviewed temperature data from various countries and cities, noting a reduction in cold-related fatalities and a rise
  in heat-related fatalities from 2000-2003 to 2016-2019, leading to an overall decrease in temperature-related
  mortality.
</p>
###The trend of heat-related fatalities
<p>
  Rising heat from climate change may soon outweigh the drop in cold-related deaths, especially as extreme temperatures
  strain <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2025/11/ultra-processed-food-health-damage-seniors.md" title="Ultra Processed Food Health Damage Seniors" rel="dofollow">**health and food**</a> systems. Most temperature-related deaths happen during moderately hot or cold weather, not
  extremes—highlighting the need for better protection. In the U.S., the safest temperature is around 24°C (75°F). The
  best defense? Stronger hearts, wider access to virus vaccines, and safe, stable housing for all.
</p>
###The Research Study: Scope and Investigators
<p>
  <a href="https://research.monash.edu/en/persons/yuming-guo">
    Yuming Guo, PhD, from Monash University in Melbourne, Australia
  </a>
  , and colleagues conducted the study using country-level mortality data, encompassing 1,847 locations in 32 countries
  on five continents, accounting for 98.5% of the global population. The highest numbers of temperature-related deaths
  were in Central Asia and Eastern Europe, with Asia experiencing a higher prevalence of excess cold-related deaths.
  </p></div>
<div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid rgb(13, 71, 161); border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;">
### **Cold Weather and Heart Disease: Unveiling the Chilling Truth**
<p>
  For many years, conventional wisdom held that cold weather presented a greater risk to heart health. Research has
  confirmed this to be true. A large-scale study by
  <a href="https://newsroom.heart.org/news/extremely-hot-and-cold-days-linked-to-cardiovascular-deaths">
    The American Heart Association
  </a>
  published in the journal Lancet Planetary Health analyzed data from over 32 million cardiovascular deaths across 567
  cities worldwide.</p><p>The findings revealed a significant association between extremely cold days and an increased risk of
  heart attack and other cardiovascular complications, particularly for individuals with pre-existing heart conditions
  like heart failure.
</p>
<p>**Research Highlights:**</p>
<p></p>
<ul>
  <li>
    A multinational study analyzing over 32 million cardiovascular deaths across four decades found that death rates
    were higher on days with the highest and lowest temperatures.
  </li>
</ul>
<ul>
  <li>
    Among various cardiovascular diseases, heart failure patients had the highest number of additional deaths during
    extreme temperature conditions.
  </li>
</ul>
<ul>
  <li>
    Researchers emphasize the need for further research to explore and devise methods to potentially reduce the effects
    of extreme temperatures on cardiovascular disease due to climate change.
  </li>
</ul>
<p></p>
###How does cold weather impact heart health?
<p></p>
<ul>
  <li>
    **Cold weather makes your heart work harder:**
    blood vessels tighten to save heat, raising blood pressure and reducing blood flow.
  </li>
  <li>
    This strain, combined with thicker blood and higher clot risk,
    **can trigger heart attacks or strokes.**
    Shivering also boosts heart rate and oxygen needs—especially risky if your heart is already weak.
  </li>
  <li>
    Stay warm, move gently, and watch for warning signs,
    **especially if you have heart disease.**
  </li>
</ul>
<p></p>

### The Heat is On: How Summer Temperatures Impact Your Heart
<p>
  Extreme heat is more dangerous than many realize—while cold weather poses a greater overall risk, hot days also raise
  the chance of heart trouble.
</p>
<p></p>
<ul>
  <li>
    **Sweating a lot can lead to dehydration**
    , reducing blood volume and making your heart work harder.
  </li>
  <li>
    **Lost electrolytes can disrupt heart rhythm,**
    increasing arrhythmia risk.
  </li>
  <li>
    **And just like in the cold, your heart is under strain**
    as it pumps blood to cool your skin—making extreme heat a serious concern, especially for those with existing heart
    conditions.
  </li>
</ul>
  <p></p></div>
<div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid rgb(13, 71, 161); border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;">
### **Extreme temperatures and the risk of heart attack: Who is most vulnerable?**
<p>Certain individuals are more susceptible to the dangers of extreme temperatures and heart health. These include:</p>
###People with Existing Heart Disease:
People with existing heart problems, like coronary artery disease, heart failure, or a past heart attack, are at higher
risk during extreme temperatures. Studies show that very hot or very cold days can greatly increase the chance of
heart-related deaths, especially for those with heart failure, who are most sensitive to temperature changes. It’s
important for anyone with heart issues to be extra careful in such weather to help avoid possible complications.
<br />
###Older Adults: The Aging Heart and Temperature Regulation
<p>
  As we get older, the heart becomes less adaptable to hot or cold weather. Shifts in body fat, thinner skin, reduced
  sweating, and stiffer heart muscles make it tougher to regulate temperature. This can put extra strain on the heart,
  especially during activity, increasing the risk of problems like diastolic failure—especially for those with high
  blood pressure or diabetes.
</p>
<p>
  Staying hydrated, layering clothing, maintaining muscle, and keeping an eye on weather changes can help older adults
  stay safe and keep their hearts healthier for longer.
</p>
<p>
  **Author Note:**
  <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2024/01/sepsis.md" title="Sepsis" rel="dofollow">**Since having sepsis**</a>, I often feel cold, and it’s hard for me to notice when I’m overheating. That’s why I wear a watch
  with heat and heart sensors, which I recommend to everyone.
</p>
###People with High Blood Pressure:
<p>
  High blood pressure puts extra stress on the heart, and extreme temperatures—hot or cold—can make it even tougher.</p><p></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li>**Heat makes your heart work harder** to cool you down, boosting heart rate and blood flow</li><li>**Cold can increase blood
  pressure a**nd stiffen blood vessels, raising the risk of serious problems.</li><li>**Staying hydrated**</li><li>**Dressing for the weather**</li><li>**Watching for symptoms **during temperature swings are important.</li></ul><p></p><p>If you have high blood pressure, touching base with
  your doctor before a heatwave or cold snap can help you stay safe.
</p>
###People with Obesity: Increased Vulnerability in Extreme Temperatures
<p>
  Extra weight means your heart works harder every day—and extreme heat makes it worse by trapping body heat and
  boosting strain. Staying cool, hydrated, and active during milder times of day can help. Pair that with medical
  support to manage conditions and medications, and you’re building real protection against temperature-related risks.
</p>
###Sleep Apnea:
Obesity is a <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2024/02/sleep-apnea.md" title="Sleep Apnea" rel="dofollow">**major cause of obstructive sleep apnea**</a> (OSA), a condition where breathing repeatedly stops during the night
due to a blocked airway. Extra weight, especially around the neck, can narrow the throat, while belly fat can reduce
lung capacity, making airway collapse more likely.</div><div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid rgb(13, 71, 161); border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;">These pauses in breathing put stress on the heart and can worsen other health problems. The cycle continues as sleep messes with hunger hormones, boosting appetite and making weight gain harder to control. The upside is that losing weight can significantly ease symptoms, break the cycle and improve both <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2025/01/sleep-and-heart-health-catching-up-on.md" title="Sleep And Heart Health Catching Up On" rel="dofollow">**sleep and heart health**</a>.</div><div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid rgb(13, 71, 161); border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;"><h3 style="text-align: left;">Inflammation:<p>
  Obesity often leads to chronic, low-grade <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2024/10/in-the-blood.md" title="In The Blood" rel="dofollow">**inflammation that damages blood vessels and raises**</a> the risk of clots.
  Extreme temperatures can make things worse—heat puts extra stress on the body and weakens immunity, while cold might
  spark natural anti-inflammatory responses that could offer some benefit.</p><p>This highlights the close connection between
  weight, inflammation, and environment, especially when it comes to heart health. Recognizing these links can help
  protect those most vulnerable during heatwaves or cold spells.
  </p></div>
<div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid rgb(13, 71, 161); border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;">
### Protecting Your Heart in All Seasons
<p>You can protect your heart year-round by:</p>
<p></p>
<ul>
  <li>**Staying hydrated**</li>
  <li>**Dressing for the weather**</li>
  <li>**Avoiding intense activity during temperature extremes.**</li>
  <li>
    **Managing conditions like high blood pressure or obesity**
    —even with small, steady improvements—makes a big difference.
  </li>
  <li>**Layer up in cold weather**</li>
  <li>
    **Cool down in heat and always listen to your body**
    .
  </li>
</ul>
<p></p>
<p>
  If you feel chest pain, dizziness, or shortness of breath, seek help right away—your heart’s telling you something
  important.
</p>
  <ul></ul></div>

  <p>
    Your heart is the body’s primary radiator. When temperatures swing, the heart has to pivot its entire mechanical
    strategy:
  </p>

### Vulnerability Profiling in 2026
  <p>
    The research by
    **Yuming Guo**
    and the
    **American Heart Association**
    highlights that we are no longer just looking at "weather," but at "adaptation capacity."
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>
      The Aging Heart: Reduced "thermal flexibility" means the heart can't speed up or slow down as efficiently as it
      once did.
    </li>
    <li>
      The Obesity Factor: Adipose tissue acts as a thermal insulator, making it harder for the heart to "dump" heat
      during summer spikes.
    </li>
    <li>
      The Inflammation Link: As we’ve seen in recent Alzheimer's research, chronic low-grade inflammation (often present
      in obesity) impairs the body's ability to handle the "cytokine storm" that can be triggered by heat exhaustion.
    </li>
  </ul></div>
<div class="box" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid rgb(13, 71, 161); border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding-left: 1rem;">
###Conclusion:
<p>
  While both hot and cold weather can pose a threat to heart health, especially for those with pre-existing conditions,
  there are proactive steps we can take to minimize the risks. By <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2023/12/Maintaining-healthy-blood-pressure.md" title="Maintaining Healthy Blood Pressure" rel="dofollow">**maintaining a healthy**</a> lifestyle, dressing
  appropriately for the weather, and staying informed about potential dangers, we can keep our hearts safe all year
  round. Remember, consulting with your doctor for personalized advice is always recommended, particularly if you have
  any concerns about your heart health.
  </p></div>
