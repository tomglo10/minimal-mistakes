<h2 style="text-align: left;">What Is Glaucoma?<p>Glaucoma is a group of eye diseases that damage the **optic nerve**, often due to increased pressure inside the eye (intraocular pressure or IOP). It is one of the **leading causes of irreversible blindness worldwide**.</p>
<p>Glaucoma typically develops slowly and without pain, which is why it’s known as the **“silent thief of sight.”**</p>
<p>Without treatment, glaucoma can lead to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Peripheral (side) vision loss</li>
<li>Tunnel vision</li>
<li>Permanent blindness</li>
</ul>
<p>Early detection is critical because vision loss from glaucoma **cannot be reversed**, but it can often be slowed or prevented.</p>
  <hr /></div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">  
<h2 style="text-align: left;">What Causes Glaucoma?<p>Glaucoma usually occurs when fluid inside the eye does not drain properly, causing pressure to build up and damage the optic nerve.</p>
<p>However, some people develop glaucoma even with normal eye pressure — a condition called **normal-tension glaucoma**.</p>
###Key Causes and Contributing Factors:
<ul>
<li>Elevated intraocular pressure</li>
<li>Poor drainage of aqueous humor</li>
<li>Reduced blood flow to the optic nerve</li>
<li>Genetic predisposition</li>
</ul>
  <hr /></div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">  
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Types of Glaucoma<h3 style="text-align: left;">1. Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (Most Common)
<ul>
<li>Develops slowly over years</li>
<li>Drainage angle remains open but inefficient</li>
<li>No early symptoms</li>
<li>Most common form in the United States</li>
</ul>
<h3 style="text-align: left;">2. Angle-Closure Glaucoma (Medical Emergency)
<p>Occurs when the drainage angle becomes suddenly blocked.</p>
<h4 style="text-align: left;">Symptoms of Acute Angle Closure:
<ul>
<li>Severe eye pain</li>
<li>Headache</li>
<li>Blurred vision</li>
<li>Halos around lights</li>
<li>Nausea or vomiting</li>
</ul>
<p>Immediate medical care is required to prevent permanent vision loss.</p>
<h3 style="text-align: left;">3. Normal-tension Glaucoma Normal-tension glaucoma is a form of glaucoma in which damage to the optic nerve occurs even though intraocular pressure remains within normal ranges. This condition can lead to progressive vision loss without the elevated eye pressure typically associated with other types of glaucoma, making early detection and monitoring essential for preserving eyesight.</div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">  
  ### 4. Secondary Glaucoma
<p>Caused by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Eye injury</li>
<li>Long-term steroid use</li>
<li>Inflammation</li>
<li>Diabetes</li>
<li>Tumors</li>
  </ul></div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">  
### 5. Congenital Glaucoma
Congenital glaucoma is a rare eye disorder that occurs at birth as a result of improper development of the eye’s drainage system. This condition leads to increased intraocular pressure, which can damage the optic nerve and impair vision.</div><div><br /></div><div>It often manifests with symptoms such as excessive tearing, light sensitivity, and an enlarged, cloudy cornea. Early diagnosis and prompt treatment, which may involve surgery to correct fluid drainage, are crucial in preventing long-term vision loss and ensuring better outcomes for affected infants.<br />
  <hr /></div>
 <div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;"> 
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Glaucoma Symptoms: What to Watch For
<p>Early glaucoma often has **no noticeable symptoms**.</p>
<p>As the disease progresses, symptoms may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gradual loss of peripheral vision</li>
<li>Difficulty seeing at night</li>
<li>Blind spots in side vision</li>
<li>Tunnel vision in advanced stages</li>
</ul>
<p>Because symptoms develop gradually, regular eye exams are essential.</p>
   <hr /></div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">   
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Who Is at Risk for Glaucoma?<p>You may be at higher risk if you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Are over age 60</li>
<li>Are African American, Hispanic, or Asian</li>
<li>Have a family history of glaucoma</li>
<li>Have high eye pressure</li>
<li>Have diabetes or high blood pressure</li>
<li>Use steroid medications long-term</li>
<li>Have thin corneas</li>
<li>Are highly nearsighted (myopia)</li>
</ul>
<p>African Americans are at significantly higher risk of earlier onset and severe vision loss.</p>
  <hr /></div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">  
<h2 style="text-align: left;">How Is Glaucoma Diagnosed?<p>A comprehensive eye exam may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>**Tonometry** – measures eye pressure</li>
<li>**Dilated eye exam** – examines optic nerve</li>
<li>**Visual field test** – detects peripheral vision loss</li>
<li>**Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)** – measures nerve fiber thickness</li>
<li>**Gonioscopy** – checks drainage angle</li>
</ul>
<p>No single test confirms glaucoma. <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2024/06/multiple-sclerosis-symptoms-diagnosis-treatment.md" title="Multiple Sclerosis Symptoms Diagnosis Treatment" rel="dofollow">**Diagnosis is based on multiple findings**</a>.</p>
  <hr /></div>
 <div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;"> 
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Glaucoma Treatment Options
<p>Glaucoma is a chronic eye condition that currently has no known cure, but with timely and appropriate treatment, its progression can be significantly slowed. The primary goal of managing glaucoma is to preserve vision and prevent further damage to the optic nerve.</p><p>Treatments often involve prescription eye drops, oral medications, laser therapy, or surgical procedures, all aimed at reducing intraocular pressure, which is the main risk factor for the disease. Early detection through regular eye exams plays a crucial role in controlling the condition, as it allows for intervention before substantial vision loss occurs.</p><p>While glaucoma cannot be reversed, consistent treatment and monitoring can help patients maintain their quality of life and protect their eyesight for many years.</p>
<h3 style="text-align: left;">1. Prescription Eye Drops (First-Line Treatment)
<p>Eye drops lower intraocular pressure by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Reducing fluid production</li>
<li>Improving drainage</li>
</ul>
<p>Common medication classes include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Prostaglandin analogs</li>
<li>Beta-blockers</li>
<li>Alpha agonists</li>
<li>Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors</li>
</ul>
<p>Consistency is <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2025/11/prevent-reverse-fatty-liver-disease-seniors.md" title="Prevent Reverse Fatty Liver Disease Seniors" rel="dofollow">**essential to prevent disease progression**</a>.</p>
<hr />
<h3 style="text-align: left;">2. Laser Treatment<ul>
<li>**Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)** for open-angle glaucoma</li>
<li>**Laser peripheral iridotomy** for angle-closure glaucoma</li>
</ul>
<p>Laser therapy may reduce or eliminate the need for drops in some patients.</p>
<hr />
<h3 style="text-align: left;">3. Glaucoma Surgery<p>If medications and laser therapy are insufficient:</p>
<ul>
<li>Trabeculectomy</li>
<li>Tube shunt surgery</li>
<li>Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery (MIGS)</li>
</ul>
<p>MIGS procedures are increasingly used for earlier-stage glaucoma due to improved safety profiles.</p>
   <hr /></div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">   
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Can Glaucoma Be Prevented?
   <p>Glaucoma cannot always be prevented, but early detection greatly reduces the risk of severe vision loss.</p>
###Prevention Strategies:
<ul>
<li>Routine comprehensive eye exams (every 1–2 years after age 40)</li>
<li>Managing blood pressure and diabetes</li>
<li>Avoiding smoking</li>
<li>Following prescribed treatment plans carefully</li>
</ul>
<hr />
<h2 style="text-align: left;">Living with Glaucoma
<p>Glaucoma is a lifelong condition requiring ongoing care.</p>
<p>Management includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Regular follow-up appointments</li>
<li>Monitoring eye pressure</li>
<li>Medication adherence</li>
<li>Lifestyle adjustments</li>
</ul>
<p>Vision rehabilitation and support services can help maintain independence if vision loss occurs.</p>
  <hr /></div>
 <div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;"> 
<h2 style="text-align: left;">The Future of Glaucoma Treatment
<p>Research is advancing in:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sustained-release drug implants</li>
<li>Neuroprotective therapies</li>
<li>Gene-based treatments</li>
<li>AI-assisted imaging for earlier detection</li>
</ul>
<p>Although lost vision cannot currently be restored, early intervention and modern therapies significantly reduce the risk of blindness.</p>
   <hr /></div>
<div class="clinic-trust-card" style="background-color: #fafafa; border-left: 6px solid #0d47a1; border-radius: 12px; margin-top: 2rem; padding: 1rem;">   
<h2 style="text-align: left;">When to See an Eye Doctor
<p>Schedule an eye exam if you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Are over age 40</li>
<li>Have a family history of glaucoma</li>
<li>Experience sudden eye pain or vision changes</li>
<li>Have diabetes or high blood pressure</li>
</ul>
<p>Early diagnosis can preserve sight.</p>
  <hr /></div>
